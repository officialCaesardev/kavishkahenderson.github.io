Greyhound Review:
At Sea in World War II, With Tom Hanks in Command

When the United States joined World War II in 1941 they were not prepared for what would happen next. They didn’t know how to get troops and supplies to the front lines quickly. Troops and equipment needed to move across the ocean, even if it meant being exposed to enemy attacks. In the Atlantic Ocean, this left Allied convoy ships vulnerable, for hours at a time, to attack by the fast advancing German submarines called U-boats.

“Greyhound’s” director, Aaron Schneider, had previously worked with Tom Hanks on “The Terminal” (2004) and “Cast Away” (2000). But “Greyhound“ was an unusual choice for him because he didn’t think it would be a good fit for Hanks. He said, “I thought I could do something really interesting and unique with it, but I just couldn’t find a way to make it work.”

The movie is adapted from “The Good Shepherd,” a 1955 novel by C.S. Forester, a popular writer of naval adventures in the 20th century (Captain Horatio Hornblower was his invention). Forester’s novel is an account of not just the action but of Krause’s shifting interior states as tension and casualties mount.

Played with the sober sturdiness we've come to expect of Hanes, Krause is a calm man of faith whose personal demeanor is modest and leadership style marked by decisiveness. He manages to keep his cool while at the samme time projecting an uneasy simmering uneasiness, even as a self described "wolf pack" of submarines bears down on his convoy

The action, though, takes precedence. And while a good deal of the film was shot on an actual boat, there is a lot CGI, and a fair amount of it is not entirely credible. "Greyhound" also feels like a movie conceived as an epic but couldn't quite muster the necessary force. As such, it's ultimately one of Hanks' most perfunctory films.
